Mycoheterotrophs and Ectomycorrhizal Community Dynamics

This project examines how fully mycoheterotrophic plants interact with ectomycorrhizal (ECM) fungi and how those interactions restructure fungal communities. Work on monotropoid species such as Sarcodes sanguinea and Corallorhiza striata demonstrates that their fungal symbionts can become highly dominant within rootball zones while ECM richness declines. These localized shifts complicate the traditional view of mycoheterotrophs as strict parasites and raise ecological questions about how fungal competition, spatial monopolization, and mycorrhizal network structure respond to these plants.

Bacterial communities across truffle life stages and close epigeous relatives

Project overview

This project examines how bacterial communities assemble and change across truffle life stages, from early development through mature sporocarps. I compare hypogeous truffle tissues with close epigeous relatives to ask whether similar host lineages support similar bacterial partners when they occupy very different ecological niches.

By combining 16S or metagenomic sequencing with detailed sampling of individual fruit bodies, the goal is to identify consistent bacterial associates, possible nitrogen-transforming guilds, and relationships between fungal development and microbial function.

Truffles in the woodrat diet

Project overview

Using ITS metabarcoding of woodrat fecal pellets collected from latrines, this project quantifies how strongly small mammals rely on hypogeous fungi in the Sierra Nevada foothills. I link truffle detection in feces to spatial patterns of truffle availability, tree host composition, and nest-level foraging behavior.

The broader goal is to understand how mycophagy by woodrats and other mammals structures belowground fungal communities through spore dispersal, and how that interaction feeds back to ectomycorrhizal plant communities.

Truffle diversity in the Sierra Nevada mountains

Project overview

Through systematic surveys with a trained truffle dog, I am building a voucher-backed inventory of truffle-forming ectomycorrhizal fungi across Sierra Nevada foothill habitats. Each collection is photographed, georeferenced, dried, and sequenced to document taxonomic diversity and host associations.

These data provide a framework for asking how truffle communities vary across elevation, soil type, and vegetation, and for connecting individual taxa to both their plant partners and their animal dispersers. The collection also serves as a foundation for future work on species descriptions, population structure, and functional traits.

Fungal endophyte communities in blue oaks and interior live oaks

Project overview

In this study, I use ITS metabarcoding to compare foliar fungal endophyte communities in blue oaks (Quercus douglasii) and interior live oaks (Q. wislizeni) across ridge and valley sites and three sampling months (May, July, September) in the Sierra Nevada foothills. The design explicitly tests the combined effects of host species, microhabitat, and season on endophyte diversity, composition, and ecological guild structure.

Interior live oaks harbor more diverse and compositionally distinct fungal communities than blue oaks, and season explains the largest share of variation in community structure. Across the growing season, blue oak communities undergo a pronounced shift: early-season assemblages are dominated by plant pathogens, but by September they are dominated by saprotrophs, particularly in valley trees. Ridge-site trees show slightly lower diversity overall, and ridge blue oaks support especially high pathogen loads early in the season. These patterns suggest that foliar endophyte communities are jointly shaped by host identity, microclimate, and seasonal stress, and that pathogen-heavy communities in blue oaks may contribute to their vulnerability under warming and drought.

Impact of cynipid gall wasps on fungal endophyte communities

Project overview

This project uses eDNA metabarcoding to characterize fungal communities in blue oak (Quercus douglasii) leaves with and without Cynipid galls, and within the galls themselves. I focus on two morphologically distinct gall types: urchin galls induced by Cynips quercusechinus and saucer galls induced by Feron gigas. By sampling ungalled leaves, galled leaves, and individual galls from the same trees, the study separates the effects of gall presence on leaf endophytes from the communities that develop inside galls.

Fungal communities in galled and ungalled leaves are broadly similar, indicating that gall formation does not strongly restructure foliar endophyte assemblages at the leaf scale. In contrast, gall tissues represent distinct microhabitats with wasp-specific fungal communities. Urchin galls have markedly reduced diversity and are strongly dominated by Cephaloascus fragrans, which is present in every urchin gall and reaches very high relative abundance. Saucer galls are more similar to leaf communities, with greater OTU overlap and less extreme dominance. PERMANOVA and indicator species analyses show that urchin and saucer galls both differ significantly from leaves and from each other, highlighting how closely related gall wasps can generate sharply divergent fungal habitats on the same host tree.

Mycoheterotrophs and Ectomycorrhizal Dominance

Scientific Summary

Mycoheterotrophic plants in the Monotropoideae obtain all of their carbon from ectomycorrhizal fungi, yet the ecological nature of this interaction remains ambiguous. In species such as Sarcodes sanguinea, the associated Rhizopogon fungus becomes highly dominant in the surrounding root zone, while most other ECM fungi are suppressed or entirely absent. This localized shift reflects strong competitive release for the Rhizopogon partner and a major restructuring of belowground fungal communities.

Unlike conventional plant–parasite systems, the morphological interface is reversed: fungal haustoria penetrate the plant’s roots, not the other way around. This unusual architecture raises the possibility that Rhizopogon may gain ecological advantages—such as spatial monopolization of host roots—even while providing carbon to the plant. The interaction may therefore be more complex than simple one-directional parasitism.

These patterns, combined with severe reductions in ECM richness within rootball zones, suggest that mycoheterotrophs act as powerful shapers of fungal community structure. Their presence can mark areas where competitive hierarchies among ECM fungi have shifted dramatically, providing a unique window into how belowground networks respond to specialized plant–fungus associations.
